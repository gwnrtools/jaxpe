## Waveform Construction and Approximants

The generation of gravitational waves from perturbed black holes relies on evolving the Newman-Penrose scalar $$\Psi_4$$. As the waves propagate to the transverse-traceless (TT) gauge of our detectors on Earth, they manifest as the metric strain components $$h_+(t)$$ and $$h_\times(t)$$. To construct accurate templates across the entire coalescing binary parameter space, researchers rely on a triad of sophisticated approximation schemes.

### Post-Newtonian (PN) Theory

During the early inspiral, where orbital velocities are small ($$v \ll c$$) and the separation is large, the two-body dynamics are expanded in a perturbative series in powers of the dimensionless velocity $$v/c$$. The gravitational-wave phase $$\Phi(f)$$ in the frequency domain is typically written as:

$$
\Phi(f) = 2 \pi f t_c - \phi_c + \frac{3}{128 \eta v^5} \sum_{k=0}^N \left( \alpha_k + \beta_k \ln v \right) v^k
$$

where $$\eta = m_1 m_2 / (m_1+m_2)^2$$ is the symmetric mass ratio, and $$v = (\pi M f)^{1/3}$$ is the characteristic orbital velocity. This expansion is essential for accurately capturing the millions of phase cycles accumulated over years of observation [1].

### Self-Force (SF) Formalism

For Extreme Mass Ratio Inspirals (EMRIs), where $$q \ll 1$$, the Post-Newtonian expansion converges slowly. Instead, the Self-Force formalism treats the smaller body as a perturbation on the exact background spacetime (e.g., Schwarzschild or Kerr) of the supermassive primary. The small body travels on a geodesic that is corrected by a local gravitational self-force $$f^\alpha$$ generated by its own back-scattered field [2]:

$$
u^\beta \nabla_\beta u^\alpha = f^\alpha
$$

### Numerical Relativity (NR)

In the final moments of coalescence, the binary enters the highly non-linear, strong-field regime where all perturbative expansions break down. Here, we must solve the full Einstein field equations numerically. This is typically achieved using the 3+1 ADM decomposition, evolving the spatial metric $$\gamma_{ij}$$ and extrinsic curvature $$K_{ij}$$ forward in time on supercomputers [3].

### `ToyChirp`

The module provides a toy time-domain waveform model used for testing and validation. It demonstrates the fundamental quadrupole radiation physics using the lowest-order Newtonian amplitude and phase evolution.

## The Frequency-Domain Likelihood

The `gw` module builds a frequency-domain likelihood from a user-supplied, JAX-differentiable time-domain waveform model and standard GW priors. Assuming the detector noise $$n(t)$$ is stationary and Gaussian with a one-sided power spectral density (PSD) $$S_n(f)$$, the likelihood of observing data $$d$$ given parameters $$\boldsymbol{\theta}$$ is governed by the Whittle likelihood in the frequency domain [4]. 

$$
\mathcal{L}(d | \boldsymbol{\theta}) \propto \exp\left[ -\frac{1}{2} (d - h(\boldsymbol{\theta}) | d - h(\boldsymbol{\theta})) \right]
$$

## Precision and Floating Point

GW likelihoods are validated in float64 (`jax.config.update("jax_enable_x64", True)`). Any float32 fast path must use segment-relative times. Absolute GPS epochs are unrepresentable at float32 resolution.
